Log

It is required to keep a log of your progress. Its purpose is to help you reflect on your work -- for instance, to interrogate your decision-making in composing or recomposing, to pay attention to surprises in listening, to question and try to account for your responses.

It may be helpful to title entries in your log with the Project, Experiment, or task.

Projects and Papers

Background

Project 1.1 is to reconstruct tunes in Impromptu.

In reconstructing tunes, make note in your log of the following structures as you hear and actually create them.

  • Hierarchical structures -- motive, phrase, section; what generates the boundaries of these structures? How do you know?
  • What "germinal motives" do you find?
    • Where/when did you find/make sequences?
    • Repetition and return: Why is "repetition" never the same? Give an example.
  • Antecedent-consequent phrase relationships; how are these phrases related to feelings of tension and resolution?
  • Simple forms--e.g. a b a', etc.

Pay attention to if and when these structural functions emerge. Jot down in your log those you hear and also those in the list that you do not find. Are some more cognitively “complex” than others? What, then, makes for greater complexity?

Instructions

For each of the block-sets you worked with in composing tunes, describe and discuss the following:

I.A. Your process in composing tunes:
  • The blocks you tried, what worked, what didn't, and why.
  • The strategies you used in making your tune. For instance: "Cumulating" (going next-next-next) Finding a good beginning and a good ending and working on the middle. Building modules that worked and putting them together.
  • Surprises you encountered – for instance: Hearing the same block in a new way as a result of context; Finding that your pre-built modules didn't always work together.
  • Initial attempts at accounting for the decisions you made along the way.
I.B. Completed tunes (Groups 1 and 2):
  • The structure of your completed tune, including a diagram (tree structure) showing functions and structural hierarchy.
  • A comparison of the structures of your tunes with those in Project 1.1
II.A Block-set Group 2:
  • What specific features do you think make these blocks "strange;” in what ways are they different from those in Group 1?
  • Why do these differences make the blocks more difficult to work with?
  • What does this "strangeness" tell you about the aspects you have come to expect (take for granted) in more familiar music?
II.B. If you made changes in pitches and/or durations, be sure to consider the following:
  • What was the problem you were trying to "fix?" what, specifically did you change?
  • How did your changes succeed in solving the problem?

Instructions

Do Experiment 1, and then analyze it (see analysis section). Do Projects 2.1 and 2.3, and write analysis about them.

Experiment 1

You will ask subjects to clap 2 rhythms and invent a notation for each.

Choose 3 people who, if possible, have differing musical backgrounds— e.g., play an instrument by ear, read music notation, neither...

Each subject will clap back 2 rhythms (see below). After successfully clapping back a rhythm, ask each subject to:

"Put on paper some way to show someone else how to clap the rhythm you just clapped."

Ask the subject to describe their notation.

Rhythms

Clapping examples are in impromptu notation, or drummer numbers.

  1. 4 4 2 2 4 4 4 2 2 4

    Be sure to do the repeat.

  2. 2 2 4 4 4 2 2 4

Further explanation of the rhythm notation

Larger numbers represent longer durations, smaller numbers represent shorter durations. The smaller the number on a drumblock, the faster events will follow one another.

The numbers on drumblocks represent durations that are proportional to one another: e.g., the 2-blocks will go twice as fast as 4-blocks; 6-block wills goes twice as slow as a 3-blocks. So the first example, 4 4 2 2 4 4 4 2 2 4, will sound like:

"one, two, shut-the-door; five, six, pick-up-sticks"

And the second example, 2 2 4 4 4 2 2 4, will sound like:

"shut-the-door; please, do, shut-the-door"

Experiment 1 Analysis

  • Subjects’ music backgrounds
  • Any problems clapping back?

  • Analyze the notation with respect to:

    • What features/aspects does the subject focus on?
    • How does the subject describe their notation?
    • Is the notation more figural or metric; why?
